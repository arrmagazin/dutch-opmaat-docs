# Passive Voice  *(B2)*

The passive voice (*lijdende vorm*) shifts the focus from **who does** the action to **what undergoes** it.

Dutch builds the passive with two different auxiliaries depending on whether you mean the **action itself** or its **finished result**:

- **worden** — the action is happening / happened (process focus)
- **zijn** — the action is already completed (state focus)

This split is the single most important thing to internalise. English collapses both into "to be" + past participle, so learners must consciously pick.

| Auxiliary | Focus | Example | English |
|-----------|-------|---------|---------|
| **worden** | the action / process | *De brief **wordt** geschreven.* | The letter **is being** written. |
| **zijn** | the result / completed state | *De brief **is** geschreven.* | The letter **has been** written. |

> **Rule of thumb:** if you can replace the English verb with "is being / was being," use **worden**. If you can replace it with "has been / had been," use **zijn**.

## Present passive — *worden* + past participle

Structure: **subject** + *worden* (V2) + … + **past participle** (end of clause).

| Active | Passive |
|--------|---------|
| *Jan **schrijft** de brief.* | *De brief **wordt** (door Jan) **geschreven**.* |
| *De kok **kookt** het eten.* | *Het eten **wordt** (door de kok) **gekookt**.* |
| *Wij **lezen** het boek.* | *Het boek **wordt** (door ons) **gelezen**.* |

The agent — the original subject of the active sentence — is introduced with **door** ("by") and is optional. Drop it whenever the doer is unimportant or unknown.

## Past passive — *werd / werden* and *is/zijn … (geworden)*

### Imperfectum passive — ongoing past action

*werd / werden* + past participle. Used for narration and description.

- *Het huis **werd** in 1920 **gebouwd**.* — The house was built in 1920.
- *De brieven **werden** elke dag **bezorgd**.* — The letters were delivered every day.

### Perfectum passive — completed result

*zijn* + past participle. Technically *zijn* + past participle + *geworden*, but **the *geworden* is dropped** in everyday Dutch.

- *De brief **is** **geschreven**.* — The letter has been written.
- *De ramen **zijn** **gewassen**.* — The windows have been washed.

> So both *Het huis is gebouwd* and *Het huis werd gebouwd* translate as "The house was built," but they don't mean the same thing:
>
> - *werd gebouwd* — talks about the **building process** (e.g. *in 1920*).
> - *is gebouwd* — talks about the **current finished state** (it stands built today).

## Future passive — *zullen* + *worden*

Structure: **zullen** (V2) + … + past participle + **worden** (end).

- *De brief **zal** morgen **geschreven worden**.* — The letter will be written tomorrow.
- *De kamers **zullen** **schoongemaakt worden**.* — The rooms will be cleaned.

The infinitive *worden* always sits at the very end of the clause, after the participle.

## Modal verbs in the passive

Same end-of-clause pattern: **modal** (V2) + … + **past participle** + **worden**.

| Active | Passive |
|--------|---------|
| *Je **moet** de brief **schrijven**.* | *De brief **moet geschreven worden**.* |
| *Je **kunt** dit boek **lezen**.* | *Dit boek **kan gelezen worden**.* |
| *Je **mag** hier **roken**.* | *Hier **mag gerookt worden**.* |

## The "*er*"-passive — no real subject

When the active sentence has no clear object (intransitive verbs, generic actions), Dutch fills the subject slot with **er**. The verb stays singular.

- *Er **wordt** gedanst.* — There is dancing. / People are dancing.
- *Er **werd** veel **gelachen**.* — There was a lot of laughter.
- *Er **wordt** aan de deur **geklopt**.* — Someone is knocking at the door.

This construction is everywhere in Dutch, especially in signs, instructions and impersonal observations:

- *Hier **wordt** Nederlands **gesproken**.* — Dutch is spoken here.
- *Er **mag** niet **gerookt worden**.* — Smoking is not allowed.

## 8. Active vs. passive — side-by-side

| Tense | Active | Passive (*worden*/*zijn*) |
|-------|--------|----------------------------|
| Present | *Jan schrijft de brief.* | *De brief **wordt** geschreven.* |
| Imperfectum | *Jan schreef de brief.* | *De brief **werd** geschreven.* |
| Perfectum | *Jan heeft de brief geschreven.* | *De brief **is** geschreven.* |
| Future | *Jan zal de brief schrijven.* | *De brief **zal** geschreven **worden**.* |
| Modal | *Jan moet de brief schrijven.* | *De brief **moet** geschreven **worden**.* |

## 9. When to use the passive

| Reason | Example |
|--------|---------|
| The doer is unknown or irrelevant | *Mijn fiets **is gestolen**.* — My bike has been stolen. |
| The action matters more than who does it | *Het rapport **wordt** vandaag **besproken**.* |
| Generic / impersonal statements (signs, rules) | *Hier **wordt** niet **geparkeerd**.* |
| Bureaucratic, formal, or scientific tone | *De resultaten **werden** in 2025 **gepubliceerd**.* |

In informal speech Dutch often prefers **men** ("one") or **je** ("you" generic) instead of a passive: *Hier spreek je Nederlands* feels lighter than *Hier wordt Nederlands gesproken*.

## Common mistakes

- **Using *zijn* where *worden* is needed** (process vs. state confusion):
  ❌ *De brief **is** nu geschreven door Jan* (sounds like it's already done)
  ✅ *De brief **wordt** nu door Jan geschreven* — The letter is being written right now.
- **Forgetting that *geworden* is dropped** in the passive perfectum:
  ❌ *De brief is geschreven geworden* → ✅ *De brief is geschreven*.
  (*geworden* is only kept in the **active** perfectum of *worden*, e.g. *Hij is dokter geworden* — He has become a doctor.)
- **Wrong word order with future / modal passives** — *worden* must be at the end:
  ❌ *De brief zal worden geschreven morgen* → ✅ *De brief zal morgen geschreven **worden***.
- **Translating English "by" with *bij*** — the agent uses **door**: *door Jan*, not *bij Jan*.
- **Trying to passivise a verb with no direct object** — use the **er**-passive instead: *Er wordt gedanst*, not *Het wordt gedanst*.

# Noun phrases  *(A2)*

A Dutch noun phrase is what you put around a noun to turn it into a usable chunk of meaning: *de auto*, *een snelle auto*, *de snelle rode auto van mijn broer*. This page shows the template that builds them.

## The template

A typical Dutch noun phrase has four slots, in order:

```
[ determiner ]  [ adjective(s) ]  [ noun ]  [ postmodifier ]
     de             snelle rode      auto      van mijn broer
```

- **Determiner**: article, demonstrative, possessive, or quantifier .
- **Adjective(s)**: zero or more attributive adjectives.
- **Noun**: the head, optionally a compound (always written as one word: *huiswerk*).
- **Postmodifier**: a prepositional phrase, a relative clause, or both.

Examples:

| Phrase | Structure |
|--------|-----------|
| *de hond* | det + noun |
| *een grote hond* | det + adj + noun |
| *die twee oude vrienden* | det + quant + adj + noun |
| *de man met de hoed* | det + noun + PP |
| *de vrouw **die ik gisteren zag*** | det + noun + relative clause |
| *een mooi cadeau **voor je verjaardag*** | det + adj + noun + PP |

## Adjective order

When you stack adjectives, Dutch order is roughly **opinion → size → age → shape → colour → origin/material → purpose**, the same intuition as English.

- *een **mooi groot oud Nederlands** huis* — a beautiful big old Dutch house

> Two-adjective stacks (*mooi groot*, *snel rood*) are common; three-adjective stacks are rare in speech and usually feel literary.

Each adjective takes the -e ending under the standard rules. The rules don't change with stacking — see [adjectives](/#/grammar?doc=2-parts-of-speech/04-adjectives.md) for the -e logic.

## Quantifiers and "before the determiner"

A small set of quantifiers sit **before** the determiner, not after it.

| Phrase | English |
|--------|---------|
| **al** de mensen | all the people |
| **al** mijn boeken | all my books |
| **heel** de dag | the whole day |
| **zo'n** dag | such a day |

> *Al* never takes -e even though it acts adjective-like. *Heel* before a determiner is invariable too: *heel de stad*. But attributive *hele* (after a determiner) follows the normal -e rule: *de hele dag*.

Also pre-determiner:

- **allebei**, **alletwee** — *allebei de kinderen* (both children)
- **wat een** — *wat een mooi huis!* (what a beautiful house)
- **zulke** / **zo'n** — *zulke mensen* / *zo'n man* (such people / such a man)

## Postmodifiers

### Prepositional phrases

The most common postmodifier. It attaches directly to the noun:

- *het boek **op tafel*** — the book on the table
- *de trein **naar Utrecht*** — the train to Utrecht
- *een kop **koffie met melk*** — a cup of coffee with milk
- *de man **uit Amsterdam*** — the man from Amsterdam

For thing-replacement, PPs convert to er-words: *het boek **erover*** = the book about it. See [er-words](/#/grammar?doc=1-auxiliaries/05-er-word.md).

### Relative clauses

A relative clause attaches after the noun and is verb-final inside:

- *de vrouw **die naast me woont*** — the woman who lives next to me
- *het huis **dat we kochten*** — the house that we bought
- *de stad **waar ik woon*** — the city where I live

See [relative clauses](/#/grammar?doc=5-sentences/04-relative-clauses.md) for the choice between *die / dat / wat / waar-*.

### Stacking postmodifiers

A noun can carry both a PP and a relative clause; PP comes first.

- *de man **met de hoed** **die daar staat*** — the man with the hat who is standing there

## Possession inside the NP

Dutch has three ways to mark possession inside a noun phrase:

- **Possessive determiner**: *mijn boek* (my book) — the everyday form.
- **van + noun**: *het boek **van Jan*** (Jan's book) — neutral, works for any noun.
- **Genitive -s on names**: *Jans boek* (Jan's book) — slightly literary; only with proper names and no apostrophe.

> Avoid `'s` on common nouns. *Het boek van de man* is right; *de mans boek* is wrong.

## NPs as subject vs. object

The phrase doesn't change shape between subject and object position — Dutch nouns have no case marking. Only **pronouns** distinguish subject/object form (see [pronouns](/#/grammar?doc=2-parts-of-speech/00-pronouns.md)).

- *De **grote hond** bijt de **kleine hond**.* — The big dog bites the little dog. (Both NPs identical in form.)

## Postpositions and circumpositions

Some Dutch prepositions can sit **after** the noun, especially with motion:

- *de berg **op*** — up the mountain
- *de trap **af*** — down the stairs
- *het bos **in*** — into the woods

A few words form **circumpositions** — one before, one after:

- *de hele dag **door*** — all day long
- *de stad **uit*** — out of the city

> These postposition uses are emphatic and concrete. The plain prepositional form (*op de berg*) describes location; the postposition form (*de berg op*) describes motion toward a goal.

## Common mistakes

- ❌ *een **rode** auto* + ❌ *een **rood** auto* mixed up → ✅ check de/het: *de* + adj-e (*de rode auto*); *een* + de-noun + adj-e (*een rode auto*); *een* + het-noun + bare adj (*een rood huis*).
- ❌ *al **de** boeken **mijn*** → ✅ *al **mijn** boeken* — possessive replaces the article; you can't stack them.
- ❌ *Jan**'s** boek* → ✅ *Jans boek* — no apostrophe on genitive names ending in a consonant.
- ❌ *de **hele** dag* meaning "all day every day" → ✅ *de hele dag* = the whole (one) day; *elke dag* = every day.
- ❌ *het boek **dat** ik over jou las* (when the relative attaches to a person) → ✅ check the antecedent: *die* for de-words and all plurals, *dat* for het-words singular.

# Dutch Word Order (SVO + V2)  *(A2)*

Dutch is sometimes called an "SVO" language, but it's more accurate to call it a **V2** language: in main clauses, the **conjugated verb** must be in **second position**, no matter what comes first.

This single rule, plus a few placement conventions, governs almost all Dutch word order.

---

## 1. The V2 Rule (Main Clauses)

In a Dutch main clause, the **finite verb is always the second element** (slot 2). What sits in slot 1 can be a subject, an object, an adverb, or a whole subordinate clause — but slot 2 is always the verb.

| Slot 1 | Slot 2 (verb) | Rest |
|--------|---------------|------|
| Ik | **werk** | morgen in Utrecht. |
| Morgen | **werk** | ik in Utrecht. |
| In Utrecht | **werk** | ik morgen. |
| Wat | **doe** | je vandaag? |

> Notice the subject (**ik**) jumps to slot 3 when something other than the subject opens the sentence. This is **inversion**.

### Why this matters

Many beginners say things like ❌ *Morgen ik werk in Utrecht* (English-style word order). Dutch requires *Morgen **werk** ik …*.

---

## 2. The Slot Model

Think of a Dutch main clause as having these positions:

| 1 (front) | 2 (V1) | 3 (subject) | 4 (mid-field: time/manner/place/object) | 5 (verb pile) |
|-----------|--------|-------------|------------------------------------------|---------------|
| Vandaag | heb | ik | met mijn vriend in het park | gewandeld. |
| Hij | wil | (Ø) | morgen in Antwerpen | werken. |
| Wij | hebben | (Ø) | de film gisteren | gezien. |

- **Slot 1**: any single element (subject, time, place, conjunction-clause, …).
- **Slot 2**: the conjugated verb. Always.
- **Slot 3**: subject if it didn't take slot 1.
- **Slot 4**: middle field — order tends to be **time → manner → place → object**.
- **Slot 5**: any non-finite verbs (participles, infinitives) and separable prefixes — always at the **end**.

---

## 3. Adverbs in the Sentence: The "Middle Field"

Adverbs sit in the *middle field* — between the conjugated verb and any non-finite verbs or objects.

| Position 1 | V2 | Subject | Adverb(s) | Object | Verb (rest) |
|------------|-----|---------|-----------|--------|-------------|
| *Ik* | *heb* | — | *gisteren* | *het boek* | *gelezen.* |
| *Vandaag* | *gaat* | *hij* | *waarschijnlijk* | — | *werken.* |

When you have multiple adverbs in slot 4, Dutch prefers **Time → Manner → Place**

| Time | Manner | Place |
|------|--------|-------|
| morgen | met de trein | naar Amsterdam |
| *gisteren* | *snel* | *naar huis* |

- *Ik ga **morgen** **met de trein** **naar Amsterdam**.*

Reorder freely for emphasis by promoting one to slot 1:

- *Morgen ga ik met de trein naar Amsterdam.* (emphasises *time*)
- *Met de trein ga ik morgen naar Amsterdam.* (emphasises *manner*)
- *Ik ben **gisteren snel naar huis** gegaan.* — I went home quickly yesterday.
- *Hij werkt **vandaag hard op kantoor**.* — He's working hard at the office today.

> English uses Place–Manner–Time (*home quickly yesterday*); Dutch is the **mirror image**. This is one of the most common interference errors.

---

## 4. The "Verb Pile" at the End

Any **non-finite verb** (past participle, infinitive) or **separable prefix** clusters at the **end** of the main clause.

| Example | English |
|---------|---------|
| Ik heb gisteren een boek **gelezen**. | I read a book yesterday. |
| Hij wil morgen voetbal **spelen**. | He wants to play football tomorrow. |
| Ze staat om zeven uur **op**. | She gets up at seven. (separable: *opstaan*) |
| Ik moet vanavond werken **gaan**. | I have to go work tonight. |

> If you have *both* a participle and an infinitive, the order is usually **modal infinitive** *then* **participle/infinitive of the main verb**: *Ik heb het **moeten doen**.*
>

## 5. Pronouns and Definite Objects Move Forward

Pronouns and definite noun objects sit **as early as possible** in the middle field — typically right after the verb or subject.

| Example |
|---------|
| Ik heb **het** gisteren **gelezen**. (pronoun first) |
| Ik heb **het boek** gisteren **gelezen**. (definite NP first) |
| Ik heb gisteren **een boek** **gelezen**. (indefinite later) |

Rule: **definite / pronoun → before** time adverb; **indefinite → after**.

## Common mistakes

- Forgetting V2 inversion when a non-subject opens the sentence.
- Putting participles in the middle: ❌ *Ik heb gelezen het boek* → ✅ *Ik heb het boek **gelezen***.
- Wrong TMP order: ❌ *Ik ga naar Utrecht met de trein morgen* → ✅ *Ik ga **morgen met de trein naar Utrecht***.
- Splitting separable verbs incorrectly: ❌ *Ik **opsta*** → ✅ *Ik **sta** om zeven uur **op***.
